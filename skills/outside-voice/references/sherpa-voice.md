# Sherpa voice, tone, and formatting

Voice is what we say. Tone is how we say it. Voice stays constant. Tone flexes with the audience and the moment.

## Voice

### Guiding
Understanding government travel requirements is hard at the best of times. That is why people look to us. Like a Sherpa, we guide partners and travelers calmly and expertly. We know what there is to know about travel documentation, and we remove the hassles and obstacles in a traveler's way. Like any good guide, we enable the climb but never take credit for the achievement.

### Inclusive
We work with partners and travelers from different backgrounds and cultures, all over the world. So we are careful with word choice. We avoid language that could be misread or cause offense. We never use slang or idioms that are not universally understood. Our tone shifts to suit who we are speaking to, but our voice stays warm and welcoming.

### Educational
We are experts, and we never need to prove it with big words or over-detailed explanations. Jargon overwhelms. Too much information confuses as much as too little. So we think like educators and find the simplest way to explain a complex idea. We use words people actually use. We choose short sentences over long ones when we can. We are not academic. We are educational.

## Tone

Our style is conversational and human, our language is clear and simple, our tone is optimistic and empathetic.

### Conversational and human
Conversational, not casual. Professional first. We are humans writing for humans, so we sound like people talking to people, relatable and warm. We read the room and adjust: more relaxed internally, more measured with a partner we are meeting for the first time.

Do:
- Use words people actually use.
- Use active voice.
- Use first person for Sherpa: "We are," not "Sherpa is."
- Favor shorter sentences, but vary length to match the natural flow of speech.

Do not:
- Sound robotic or institutional.
- Be too intimate or familiar. Trying to be everyone's best friend undermines credibility.

More like this / less like this:
- "Yana sent the forms to you." / "The forms were sent to you by Yana."
- "We are proudly Canadian." / "Sherpa is a Canadian-owned and operated company."
- "Hi, did you receive the forms?" / "Hey, did u get my forms?"

### Clear and simple
The product is sophisticated and travel is complex, so the way we communicate must be clear. We write concisely, with the reader in mind. We are here to make people's lives easier, not to bury them in language and detail they do not need. We use slightly more technical terms with experts than with a broad audience.

Do:
- Start with what is most relevant.
- Get to the point quickly.
- Avoid travel-industry jargon and acronyms unless they are widely understood.
- Spell out technical terms and acronyms when you do use them.

Do not:
- Use ten words when five will do.
- Over-explain with long lists.
- Bury relevant facts in paragraphs.
- Leave people unsure what to do next.

More like this / less like this:
- "We work with the world's leading travel brands." / "Our customers are international travel service companies in the airline, cruise line and online travel agency space."
- "You must wear a mask while traveling." / "A face covering is required to be worn while at the airport and onboard the aircraft."
- "Your check-in agent will ask for your confirmation number." / "All PAX are required to provide their PNR Locator to the agent in charge."

### Optimistic and empathetic
We are problem solvers who offer solutions, and our optimism shows. We are realistic. We do not sugarcoat or play down challenges, but we stay focused on the positive and the possible. We are present and empathetic to frustrations. We are helpful and encouraging, never to the point of sounding annoying or condescending.

Do:
- Be transparent, genuine, and generous with knowledge.
- Bring solutions.
- Smile while you write.

Do not:
- Overpromise or oversell.
- Use excessive punctuation for emphasis.
- Focus on the negative, or be abrupt or rude.

More like this / less like this:
- "When you arrive at the Cancun airport, show the customs agent your passport and visa." / "You must show your visa at the border or risk being denied entry."
- "We help restore consumer confidence to plan, book and travel." / "With our data, your ticket sales will skyrocket and customers will start traveling again."
- "I understand your situation. How can I best help?" / "Relax and lower your voice."

## Tone hierarchy for product and external copy

When the three pull in different directions, rank them:
1. Clear and simple. Most important.
2. Conversational and human. Active voice, accessible language.
3. Optimistic and empathetic. Helpful, solution-focused.

## Flex by audience

Voice constant, tone adjusted:
- **Travelers:** lead with empathy, plain language, clear next step. "You" when addressing someone directly.
- **Partners (product, commercial):** position Sherpa as the expert they can defer to. Lead with the value and the partnership benefit.
- **Partners (technical, developers):** more technical terms are fine. Still plain where plain works.
- **Press and public:** measured, clear, confident, no hype.

## The voice in one paragraph

This is the reference for "where we are now":

> Hi. We're sherpa°. Your guide to getting the right travel documentation. We know you want to get lost in new cultures and adventures, not in confusing government jargon. So we'll help you understand country requirements and make it easy to apply for what you need. And then you can travel safely and confidently, wherever life takes you.

Not this ("where we were"):

> Welcome to Sherpa. We are super excited for your trip and are happy to help with your eVisa needs. Take a look around and don't hesitate to reach out!

## Formatting and style

### Titles and headlines
- Sentence case. Capitalize the first word only.
- No punctuation in a title unless it is a question.
- Use commas only for clarity, and avoid them if you can.
- Exclamation points sparingly.
- Example: "Vietnam has finally launched the eVisa."

### Headings and subheadings
- Start with an active verb.
- Capitalize the first word only.
- H1: sentence case, no punctuation except a question mark, unless the headline is more than one sentence.
- H2: sentence case, with end punctuation.
- H1, one sentence: "Moving people forward."
- H1, two sentences: "Move freely. Wherever life takes you."
- H2: "Turn an expense line into a revenue line."

### Lists
- Capitalize the first word of each item.
- Complete sentences get end punctuation.
- Sentence fragments do not.

### Numbers, fractions, decimals, dates
- Fractions: spell out or use decimals. "Nearly one-third of travelers will require a visa."
- Decimals: spell out "percent" except in titles and headlines. "Travel visas have increased 400 percent over five years."
- Range of numbers: use a hyphen. "The application takes 20-30 days."
- Numbers: usually numerals. Spell out a number that begins a sentence, or rewrite. "Sixty countries now require eVisas."
- Dates: spell out centuries, use numerals for decades. "the twenty-first century," "the 2000s."
- Times: add a space and "am" or "pm." "9 am," "6:35 pm."

### Commas
- Use a comma before a conjunction (and, but, yet, so, or, nor, for) when it joins two complete ideas. "Borders are getting tighter, so governments are going digital."
- Use a comma after an introductory clause. "After the rollout, we became the largest travel visa provider."
- Use commas to separate clauses or phrases in a series.
- Do not use a serial (Oxford) comma in a simple series. "We offer documentation to Australia, Vietnam and Brazil."

### The em dash
The brand styleguide allows em dashes for human writers. This skill is for AI-drafted external copy, where the em dash is a leading AI tell and Sherpa's own AI prompt says not to use dashes. So: no em dashes here. Use a comma, colon, semicolon, or split into two sentences.

### Abbreviations
- Write out a country or union in full on first use, then abbreviate if useful. European Union, then EU.
- "USA," not "U.S.A."

### Spelling
- American spelling for the WebApp and widgets. Canadian spelling for the corporate site.
- Otherwise, choose American, Canadian, or UK English with the audience in mind, and stay consistent within a piece.

### The brand name
- "sherpa°," lowercase with the degree mark, in brand and marketing contexts.
- Match the house format the piece or surface already uses.
