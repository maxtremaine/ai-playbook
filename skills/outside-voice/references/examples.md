# Examples: Before and after

Worked transformations in Sherpa's domain. Each "before" is AI-slop. Each "after" removes the tells, applies Sherpa's voice, and gives the piece a spine. The "before" versions are examples of what to avoid.

---

## 1. LinkedIn post (founder voice)

**Before:**
> Here's the thing about travel today: the visa is broken. Not because the technology is hard, but because nobody owns the experience. In today's fast-paced travel landscape, airlines are leaving real money on the table, money that could fundamentally transform their ancillary strategy. The implications are significant. Let that sink in.

**After:**
> Entry requirements are the last part of the trip airlines still hand back to the passenger to sort out alone. That is a strange place to stop, because it is also where airlines could be earning. We work with more than 40 carriers to turn the visa from a moment of passenger anxiety into a product they sell at checkout. The requirement does not change. Who guides the traveler through it does.

What changed: cut the "Here's the thing" opener, the "not because X, but because Y" contrast, "in today's landscape," "fundamentally," and the "the implications are significant / let that sink in" emphasis stack. Led with the promise, carried one idea, fenced it (most tools digitize; we do this specific thing), ended on the point instead of a wave. Kept it warm and human.

## 2. Partner outreach email

**Before:**
> Hi Serdar,
>
> Sherpa is a leading provider of innovative travel documentation solutions. We would love to explore potential synergies and leverage our platform to help Turkish Airlines navigate the complex regulatory landscape. A meeting could be scheduled at your convenience to unpack the opportunities.

**After:**
> Hi Serdar,
>
> We help airlines turn entry requirements into a smoother step for passengers and a new line of ancillary revenue. We are already live with carriers across all three alliances, and I think there is a clear fit with where Turkish Airlines is taking retailing.
>
> Could we find 30 minutes in the next two weeks? I will bring specifics on what an integration could look like for you.

What changed: dropped "leading provider of innovative solutions," "synergies," "leverage," "navigate," "unpack," and the "complex regulatory landscape." Fixed the passive "a meeting could be scheduled." Switched from "Sherpa is" to "we." Stated the value plainly and gave one clear next step.

## 3. Blog post introduction

**Before:**
> Here's what most people get wrong about borders. What if the real problem isn't the rules, but the way we explain them? It's not a technology problem. It's not a policy problem. It's a communication problem. And that's okay, because it's solvable.

**After:**
> Most travelers do not find out they need a visa until it is almost too late, usually from a government website written for lawyers. The rules are not the hard part. Finding out which ones apply to you, in time, in plain language, is. That gap is exactly where an airline can help, and it is what we built sherpa° to close.

What changed: removed the "Here's what most people get wrong" opener, the "What if" rhetorical setup, the negative-listing run ("it's not X, it's not Y"), and the "and that's okay" permission line. Opened with a concrete picture of the traveler, fenced the real problem, and made a promise the rest of the post can deliver on.

## 4. Press boilerplate / "About Sherpa"

**Before:**
> Sherpa is a cutting-edge, AI-powered travel-tech company revolutionizing the way travelers navigate the ever-evolving global mobility landscape through innovative, best-in-class solutions that empower seamless cross-border journeys.

**After:**
> sherpa° is a travel documentation platform that helps airlines and their passengers handle visas and entry requirements. We are integrated with more than 40 airlines worldwide, across all three major alliances, and we turn a step travelers used to dread into a smoother experience and a new source of revenue for our partners.

What changed: cut "cutting-edge," "AI-powered," "revolutionizing," "ever-evolving," "innovative, best-in-class," "empower seamless." Replaced the hype with what the company does and a fact a reader can check. (If a wire service requires third person, keep the same plainness and drop the "we.")

## 5. Conference abstract (FTE / Phocuswright style)

**Before:**
> This session will explore the evolving landscape of ancillary revenue and delve into how AI is reshaping the future of air travel. We'll unpack key trends and take a deep dive into the opportunities and challenges that lie ahead for forward-thinking airlines.

**After:**
> Airlines have built ancillary revenue on bags and seats for years. Entry documentation is the line item most have never tried. This session shows what changes when an airline sells the visa the way it sells a seat: how passengers respond, what it earns, and what it takes to set up. You will leave with a number and a starting point, not a trend report.

What changed: removed "explore the evolving landscape," "delve into," "unpack," "deep dive," "lie ahead," "forward-thinking." Made one promise, named the surprise (the untouched line item), and told the reader exactly what they will walk away with.

---

## 6. Web headline and subhead

Applies the formatting rules: sentence case, active-verb heading, no title punctuation, no em dash.

**Before:**
> Revolutionizing Cross-Border Travel — The Future of Documentation Is Here!

**After:**
> Headline: Turn an expense line into a revenue line.
> Subhead: We help airlines guide passengers through visas and entry requirements, so a moment of friction becomes a smoother trip and a new source of revenue.

What changed: dropped the title-case, the em dash, the exclamation point, and "revolutionizing / the future is here." Used a real Sherpa headline pattern, started the heading with a verb, and kept the subhead clear and warm.
