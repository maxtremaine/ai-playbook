# Clarity principles

Structural principles for longer or argued pieces: blog posts, bylines, talk scripts, partner narratives. Drawn from Patrick Winston's "How to Speak" and "Make It Clear." The lecture is about speaking; the book extends the same ideas to writing. They give a piece its spine, the way Sherpa's voice gives it its sound.

Winston's frame: how well your ideas land is mostly a function of how clearly you communicate them, which is a skill you practice, not a talent you are born with. Treat every external piece as a chance to make one idea land cleanly.

## Open with a promise

Do not warm up. Do not open with a joke or a scene-setting paragraph the reader has to wade through. Open by telling the reader what they will know or be able to do by the end. Give them a reason to keep reading.

For Sherpa, the promise is usually the value: what the partner gains, what the traveler can now do, what the reader will understand that they did not before. This matches Sherpa's own rule to start with what is most relevant.

- Weak: "Travel has changed a lot in recent years. Let's talk about visas."
- Promise: "Entry requirements are the one part of the journey airlines still hand back to the passenger to figure out alone. Here is how to change that, and turn it into revenue."

## Carry one idea, and cycle it

Decide the single thing the reader should leave with. Build the whole piece around it. Then say it more than once, in different forms, across the piece. Readers drift; a fraction miss any given sentence. Cycling back to the core idea is how you make sure it lands without sounding like repetition.

This is not the same as the AI tic of restating the thesis in a boilerplate conclusion. Cycle by showing the idea from a new angle each time: state it, illustrate it with an example, then close on what it means for the reader.

## Fence the idea

Once you state your point, distinguish it from the obvious neighbor so the reader knows what is specifically ours and does not file it under something they have already heard.

- Without a fence: "Sherpa helps airlines with digital transformation." (Sounds like every vendor.)
- With a fence: "Plenty of tools digitize the airline. Sherpa does one specific thing none of them do: it turns the visa requirement, the part passengers dread, into a product the airline sells at checkout."

## Signpost so a skimmer stays found

Most external readers skim. Give them landmarks to climb back on when their attention returns. This is Winston's verbal punctuation, applied to a page:
- A first sentence per section that carries the section's point.
- Headings that start with an active verb and say something (Sherpa's heading rule).
- Structure a reader can follow at a glance.

Signposting is not meta-commentary. "In this section we will explore" is a tell to cut. A heading that states the point is a signpost to keep.

## Use a concrete example over an abstraction

People think in stories and remember concrete things. A specific example, a real traveler at a real border, a named airline outcome, lands harder than an abstract claim.

Winston's shorthand for what makes an idea stick, the five S's: a Symbol or image, a Slogan or memorable phrasing, a Surprise (a common assumption that turns out to be false), a Salient detail that sticks out, and a Story. Reach for at least one. For Sherpa, the surprise is often the scale of the problem or the size of the missed revenue.

## Ask a real question, sparingly

A genuine question can pull a reader in. Keep it real and specific, and do not answer it in the next breath. This is different from the rhetorical-setup tell ("What if I told you..."), which the AI-tell catalog cuts. One earns engagement; the other performs it.

## Finish on the contribution, not a wave

Do not end with "Thanks for reading," "Reach out with any questions," or a limp summary. Winston calls the reflexive "thank you" a trite ending. End on what the reader should take away or do next: the value, the takeaway, the one clear step. For Sherpa, that is usually the partner's opportunity or the traveler's next move, said plainly.

## Cut the noise

Few words. No clutter. Every sentence that does not move the idea forward is noise. This is the same instinct as Sherpa's clear-and-simple rule and the density line in the score. When in doubt, cut.
