# Common Clauses: Standard Company-Side Positions

Use this as a fallback reference when the project doesn't have a template covering a given issue, or when explaining to the user *why* a given clause matters. These are general market-standard positions for a company selling a B2B service or platform. The project's own templates and stated positions always take precedence over what's written here.

## Limitation of liability

**Preferred position (selling/licensor side):** Total aggregate liability capped at fees paid in the 12 months prior to the event giving rise to the claim. Mutual cap. Exclusion of indirect, incidental, special, consequential, exemplary, and punitive damages, and of lost profits, revenue, goodwill, business interruption, and substitute procurement costs. Carve-outs for indemnification obligations, breach of confidentiality, fraud or willful misconduct, and the counterparty's payment obligations.

**Common counterparty pushes:**
- Uncap the cap entirely → reject for vendors; for a strategic enterprise customer consider a 2x or 3x fees multiplier.
- Uncap specific categories (data breach, IP infringement, gross negligence) → negotiate; if accepting, define narrowly with super-cap (e.g., 3x fees).
- Make exclusion of consequentials non-mutual → reject; mutuality is standard.
- Add carve-outs for the counterparty's gross negligence → negotiate.

**What's a red flag:** Caps below 12 months' fees. Unilateral caps that protect only the counterparty. Removing the consequential-damages exclusion entirely. Adding "this section does not apply to Section [indemnification]" without a super-cap.

## Indemnification

**Preferred position (selling side):** Indemnify the customer/partner for third-party IP infringement claims arising from use of the service as authorized. Customer/partner indemnifies for misuse, combination with non-authorized materials, modifications, use of customer data, and breaches of the AUP. Defense controlled by indemnifying party. Carve-outs for indemnified party's settlement without consent.

**Common counterparty pushes:**
- Expand indemnity to "any losses arising from the service" → reject; this is indemnification, not insurance.
- Add indemnity for data breaches → negotiate; tie it to breach of specific data-security obligations rather than any breach.
- Add indemnity for regulatory violations → negotiate; limit to violations arising from the company's own acts.
- Remove the customer/partner's reciprocal indemnity → reject.

**What's a red flag:** Indemnification "from any and all claims" with no scope. No carve-outs for customer fault. No defense rights for the indemnifying party.

## IP ownership and license grants

**Preferred position:** Company retains all right, title, and interest in its services, materials, tools, and any modifications/improvements. Customer/partner retains its data. Feedback is freely usable by the company without obligation. Aggregated/de-identified data may be used by the company for product improvement and analytics.

**Common counterparty pushes:**
- Claim ownership of "outputs" or "derived data" → negotiate scope carefully; outputs from the company's service are typically the company's.
- Remove the feedback license → reject; feedback license is industry standard.
- Restrict use of aggregated/de-identified data → negotiate; sometimes restrict to "for purposes of providing and improving the services."

## Data protection

**Preferred position:** Separate DPA. Clarify controller/processor roles. Sub-processor list with notice-and-objection rights. Breach notification "without undue delay" (not a fixed hour count for the company; demand 72 hours from the counterparty if it's a customer-side obligation). Audit rights limited (once per year, 30 days' notice, no access to other customer data, NDA required).

**Common counterparty pushes:**
- Demand on-site audit rights with short notice → negotiate strictly.
- Demand breach notification within 24 hours → push to 72 hours or "without undue delay."
- Strict liability for sub-processor acts → mutual indemnity is reasonable; strict liability with no cap is not.
- Restrict international transfers / require data residency → assess feasibility; if accepting, scope to specific data categories.

## Term and termination

**Preferred position:** Fixed initial term (1-3 years). Auto-renewal in 1-year increments unless notice given 60-90 days before renewal. Termination for material breach with 30 days' cure period (10 days for payment breach). Termination for insolvency immediate. No termination for convenience by the customer/partner mid-term.

**Common counterparty pushes:**
- Add termination for convenience → reject for short terms; for multi-year terms negotiate with notice period and refund of unused prepaid fees only.
- Shorten notice for non-renewal → resist; 60-90 days is standard and protects revenue forecasting.
- Remove auto-renewal → negotiate; if accepting, raise prices slightly or shorten initial term.

## Payment

**Preferred position:** Net 30. Late interest at 1.5%/month or the legal maximum. Disputed amounts must be raised within 30 days of invoice; undisputed portions still payable. Fees may be increased annually with 60 days' notice. All taxes excluded from fees.

**Common counterparty pushes:**
- Net 60 or Net 90 → negotiate; common with large enterprises; if accepting, build into pricing.
- Remove late interest → resist; this is leverage.
- Require fee freezes for entire term → negotiate; cap annual increase at a CPI-tied number rather than freezing.
- Add right to offset against amounts owed by the company → resist; offset rights are dangerous.

## Confidentiality

**Preferred position:** Mutual. Standard exceptions (already known, publicly available, independently developed, received from a third party free to disclose). Use limited to performance of the agreement. Survives termination, typically indefinitely for trade secrets and 3-5 years for other Confidential Information. Return or destroy on termination with carve-out for backups and legal hold.

**Common counterparty pushes:**
- Perpetual confidentiality for all Confidential Information → reject for ordinary CI; OK for trade secrets.
- Remove the backup/legal-hold carve-out → reject; technically impossible to comply with.
- Add residuals clause (information retained in unaided memory is freely usable) → assess; common in tech but cuts against the discloser.

## Assignment and change of control

**Preferred position:** Company can assign freely (including to affiliates and in connection with M&A). Counterparty cannot assign without consent. Change of control of counterparty triggers consent right (helpful when counterparty is acquired by a competitor).

**Common counterparty pushes:**
- Make assignment mutual → negotiate; consent rights are useful but commonly mutual in market.
- Remove change-of-control trigger → reject if competitor concern is real; otherwise negotiate.

## Exclusivity

**Preferred position:** Avoid exclusivity from the company side. If granting, scope tightly to a specific service category and geography, time-limit it, and tie to performance milestones (volume, revenue) with the right to convert to non-exclusive if missed.

**Common counterparty pushes:**
- Broad category exclusivity with no performance hurdle → reject; this is free option value for the counterparty.
- Mutual exclusivity → assess based on the deal; usually asymmetric in the company's favor is appropriate (exclusive to them, non-exclusive for the company).

## Publicity, marks, and references

**Preferred position:** Each party may identify the other as a customer/partner and use the other's marks for that purpose, subject to brand guidelines. Press releases mutually approved. Case studies require consent.

**Common counterparty pushes:**
- Block any use of the counterparty's name → resist; reference rights are standard and have marketing value.
- Require approval for every mention → negotiate to "marketing materials and press releases only."

## Governing law and venue

**Preferred position:** The company's home jurisdiction. Exclusive venue in the company's home city. Carve-out for the company to seek injunctive relief in any venue.

**Common counterparty pushes:**
- Their home jurisdiction → negotiate; common compromise is the defendant's jurisdiction or a neutral one (e.g., Delaware, New York, London).
- Add arbitration → assess; arbitration can be faster but limits discovery and appeal.

## Insurance

**Preferred position:** Maintain commercially reasonable insurance. Provide certificates on request. Resist specific dollar minimums in the contract — they age poorly.

**Common counterparty pushes:**
- Specify minimum coverage amounts → negotiate to reasonable industry numbers (GL $1M/$2M, professional liability $5M, cyber $5M typical for B2B SaaS).
- Name counterparty as additional insured → assess feasibility with the broker.

## Warranties

**Preferred position:** Disclaim all implied warranties (merchantability, fitness, non-infringement) in capital letters. Express warranty limited to "services will be performed in a professional and workmanlike manner" and that the service materially conforms to documentation. Sole remedy for warranty breach is re-performance or refund.

**Common counterparty pushes:**
- Demand express warranty of fitness for purpose → reject; this is an open-ended commitment.
- Remove the disclaimer of implied warranties → reject; this is the most important warranty provision in the contract.

## Force majeure

**Preferred position:** Excuses performance for events beyond reasonable control. Always carve out the obligation to pay money — payment obligations should not be excused by force majeure.

**Common counterparty pushes:**
- Add pandemic, cyber attack, supply chain disruption explicitly → fine; these are now standard.
- Excuse payment obligations under force majeure → reject; this turns a force majeure clause into a free option to stop paying.
