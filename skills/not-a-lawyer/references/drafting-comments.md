# Drafting Comments and Redlines

Guidance for when the user asks for **comments**, **redline language**, or an **email response** to send back to the counterparty.

## Audience

The reader is almost always another lawyer or someone working with a lawyer. They will read closely, respond to substance, and ignore performance. The tone they respect is collegial, direct, reasoned, and unhurried. Adversarial posturing slows deals down without changing outcomes.

## Tone

- **Direct, not deferential.** "We need X" or "Please move to X" — not "We were hoping you might consider X."
- **Reasoned, not assertive.** Every push should carry a short rationale. "We can't accept uncapped liability for the customer's own data — we have no control over what's submitted to us. Propose a super-cap of 3x annual fees instead."
- **Concise.** A comment that takes more than three sentences usually has two ideas in it; split them.
- **Specific.** Reference section numbers, defined terms, and quoted language. Avoid "this clause" when "Section 12.3" is available.
- **Neutral on motive.** Don't accuse the counterparty of being unreasonable. Address the text.

## When drafting a comment

Comments embedded in a redlined document or in a tracked-change document follow this rough structure:

> [What's wrong, in one sentence]. [Why it matters / what it does, in one sentence]. [Proposed direction or specific replacement language, in one sentence].

**Example:**
> The cap of $50,000 is far below industry standard for a deal of this size. A 12-month-fees cap is what's market for B2B SaaS at this ARR. Propose returning to the original "12 months of fees paid in the period preceding the claim" formulation.

Do not preface every comment with "We appreciate your changes, however..." Skip the warmth — that goes in the cover email if anywhere.

## When drafting redline language

When proposing replacement clause text, present it as a clean side-by-side:

> **Section [X.X] — Proposed replacement**
>
> *Current draft:*
> "[Counterparty's language]"
>
> *Proposed replacement:*
> "[Company's preferred language]"
>
> *Rationale:* [One or two sentences.]

If the proposed language deviates from the project's template, say so explicitly: "This adds a carve-out we don't have in our standard template, but is responsive to the counterparty's stated concern about [issue]."

## When drafting a cover email

The cover email summarizing the company's response to counterparty redlines should:

1. Open with one short paragraph acknowledging receipt and confirming the company has reviewed.
2. List the issues by category — what the company can accept, what needs to be negotiated, what the company can't accept and why.
3. Close with a clear next step (e.g., "We've attached our redline. Available for a call this week if helpful.").

Don't bury the can't-accepts. The counterparty's lawyer will skim — make sure the must-changes are visible above the fold.

## Avoid these patterns

- **"This is not market."** Without explaining what *is* market and why, it's a non-argument.
- **"We never agree to this."** Either it's true and irrelevant (your boilerplate is your starting position, not a commitment), or it's untrue and unhelpful.
- **Long quotations of the counterparty's own language back at them.** They wrote it. Reference it briefly and respond.
- **Multiple alternative proposals in one comment.** Pick one. If two are genuinely acceptable, lead with the stronger one and mention the fallback in a separate comment or in the email.
- **Comments that explain the law to opposing counsel.** They know.

## Examples of strong versus weak comments

**Weak:**
> We are uncomfortable with this language. Our standard position is to limit liability. Can we please revisit this section together?

**Strong:**
> Section 11.1 — The deletion of the liability cap creates uncapped exposure that we can't accept for a deal at this fee level. Propose reinstating the original 12-months-fees cap, with carve-outs only for confidentiality breach, IP indemnification, and fraud/willful misconduct.

---

**Weak:**
> The data terms are very one-sided. We would like more balance here.

**Strong:**
> Section 7 — As drafted, the company carries all the data-security obligations while the customer has none. Propose making the security and breach-notification obligations mutual, and adding a customer obligation to provide accurate sub-processor instructions before transmitting personal data.

---

**Weak:**
> We don't think we can give you exclusivity.

**Strong:**
> Section 4.1 — Worldwide exclusivity across all service categories isn't something we can offer. We can propose exclusivity scoped to [specific service] in [specific region] for [specific term], with the right to convert to non-exclusive if [counterparty] doesn't hit [volume/revenue] milestones in year one. Happy to discuss alternative structures on a call.
